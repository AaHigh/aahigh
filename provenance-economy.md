# The Provenance Economy

### Native Multimodality, the Hightower Derivative, and the Case for Universal High Income

*A the High Tower District report — June 2026*

-----

## 1. Reframing the question

The displacement debate is usually posed badly. “Will AI take the jobs?” presumes that value lives in tasks, and that whoever performs the task captures the value. But value does not live in tasks. Value lives in scarcity. The only durable economic question is therefore: **when the marginal cost of synthetic output goes to zero, where does scarcity relocate?**

Everything below is an attempt to answer that one question honestly, and to derive — from a single trend, native multimodality — a prediction about where human income comes from on the other side.

## 2. Native multimodality is the last silo falling

For three years the human economic shelter against automation was modality-shaped. Text fell first. Then images. Then audio, then video. At each stage the displaced retreated into the modality the machines had not yet absorbed, and called it “the part that requires a human.”

Native multimodality ends that retreat. A model like ERNIE 5.0 collapses text, image, video, and audio into one autoregressive representation space rather than stitching specialist models together. MiniMax M3 ships the same convergence into a million-token context at roughly one-twentieth the per-token compute of the prior generation. And the genuine frontier is no longer perception at all — it is **action as a first-class modality**: Vision-Language-Action models that reason about future states and emit low-level control, the architecture beneath embodied robotics.

That progression matters because it is *terminal*. Once action joins text, image, audio, and video inside a single representation space, there is no remaining category of output where “the AI can’t do this yet” provides shelter. The silos are gone. Native multimodality is not merely a capability upgrade; it is the **completion of the commoditization of synthetic output across every channel simultaneously**.

This is the load-bearing observation of the entire report. When supply of synthetic output becomes infinite and free in *every* modality at once, scarcity cannot relocate to another modality — because there isn’t one. It has to relocate somewhere structurally different.

## 3. The Hightower Derivative read

The control-systems framing makes the relocation legible. Treat the cognitive-output economy as a PID loop:

- **P — the proportional term, present output.** This is the thing native multimodality drives to zero cost. Any output that can be specified by its present description — a paragraph, a render, a track, a motion plan — is now cheap in all modalities.
- **I — the integral term, accumulated verified history.** This is the *ledger* of who actually did what, over time, signed and non-repudiable. As P collapses, the integral does not collapse with it. It is the one quantity in the loop that machines cannot self-generate, because the integral’s value is precisely that it records a real chain of custody back to a real origin.
- **D — the derivative term, the rate of change.** This is what AI actually contributes. Per the Hightower Derivative, AI does not displace thinking; it changes the *rate of change* of cognitive output. It accelerates the loop. But a derivative operates on a signal it did not originate. It cannot set its own setpoint.

The standard displacement narrative is a claim that AI zeroes out P and therefore zeroes out the human. The control-systems reading says something sharper and more hopeful: as P → 0, **the integral term appreciates**. The verified human ledger becomes the scarce, rising-value asset precisely *because* synthetic present-output became worthless. The faster and cheaper the machines render the present, the more the authenticated record of genuine human origin is worth.

Native multimodality, in other words, is the strongest argument ever assembled *for* a proof-of-human economy — because it removes every other place the value could have gone.

## 4. Three camps on income, and why none of them is Universal High Income

The policy conversation has fractured into three positions. It is worth being precise about all three, because the framework here belongs to none of them.

**Camp one — the cash floor (classic UBI).** Pay every person a subsistence amount for existing. This is the position Altman funded for a decade and has now walked back. Its deepest critic is Lanier, whose objection is not fiscal but dignitary: a world where everyone is on basic income is a world where everyone has been told they are economically unnecessary. The floor keeps people alive and tells them they are useless in the same gesture.

**Camp two — the ownership stake (universal basic compute / sovereign wealth fund).** Give every person a share of the machine — compute credits, or equity in a public fund that AI firms endow. This is Altman’s revised position, and there is real momentum behind a sovereign-wealth-style vehicle that passes AI gains to citizens as dividends. It is better than the floor: it makes people rentiers of the automated economy rather than its wards. But it is still passive. It rewards you for *holding a claim*, not for *doing anything*. It is dignity-neutral at best.

**Camp three — data dignity (Lanier / Weyl).** Pay people for verifiable contribution to the systems — and structure the payment so that you “benefit only when you reach out into the uncharted wilderness to do something novel.” This is the right shape. But as proposed it is still framed as compensation for *data*, and it has never had a deployable metering layer. There has been no cheap, deterministic, operator-independent way to prove that a specific human did a specific novel thing and meter the payment accordingly. So data dignity has remained an essay, not an economy, for eight years.

**Universal High Income** is the fourth position, and it is distinguished by two words.

It is *universal* because the right to earn it is open to everyone — there is no credential gate, no profession that monopolizes it.

It is *high*, not *basic*, because it is not a floor under the displaced. It is a price on the appreciating asset. As synthetic output inflates toward infinity, verified human signal deflates in supply and therefore *appreciates in price*. A subsistence floor is what you pay people you have decided are worthless. A high income is what the market pays for a scarce, rising-value input. The word “basic” concedes the premise that the human has been beaten. The word “high” asserts the opposite: that the human has become the one thing the system cannot manufacture, and is therefore worth more, not less.

Universal High Income is income for occupying the integral term — for being the authenticated origin of novel, verified human work that the accelerating machine requires and structurally cannot self-originate. It rewards humans for “the components they are properly relegated for”: setting the setpoint, doing the genuinely new thing, and being the non-repudiable origin of record. The math meters it and pays it. The operator does not, and cannot, because the entire point is that no operator’s say-so is the trusted party.

## 5. This is already happening — the integral is already appreciating

The prediction would be speculative if the market weren’t already pricing it. It is.

The clearest signal is the reframing of provenance as an asset class: value migrating from the *quality* of output to the *origin* of output, producing a bifurcated economy — a high-volume, low-cost AI layer beneath a low-volume, high-value layer powered by verified humans. Verified-human content is being described, without irony, as the coming luxury good, on the straightforward logic that scale has erased scarcity and scarcity is what creates value. Verification infrastructure — biometric human-content stamps, proof-of-human protocols, provenance ledgers — is being built right now to gatekeep feeds and command premium ad inventory.

Read against your own prior work, this is not a surprise. CELLTOWER was a proof-of-human-work platform with cryptographically signed score receipts before “authenticity as an asset class” had a name. The 1999 Rush Wavenet receipt — a SHA-derived token verifying a legitimate human score before upload — was the integral term, built by hand, twenty-seven years early. The thesis was never about Tetris. It was that the math should be the trusted party, not the operator, and that a signed record of genuine human work is the thing that holds value when everything cheap has been automated. The market is now arriving at that conclusion on its own.

## 6. Prediction

Stated with the restraint the subject deserves — and with the honest downside branch, because a prediction without a failure mode is marketing.

**Near term (2026–2028).** No AGI milestone resolves the definitional argument; the forecasters compress timelines and the skeptics hold, and both keep being partly right because “AGI” is a moving setpoint, not an event. Native multimodality becomes table stakes — every serious model is unified across modalities, and VLA/embodied systems move from demo to deployment. Proof-of-human and content-provenance tooling crosses from novelty to infrastructure. The income debate stays deadlocked between the cash floor and the ownership stake, because the data-dignity position still lacks a metering layer. Universal High Income remains, in this window, an argument rather than a payroll.

**Medium term (2028–2032).** The decisive event is not a model release. It is the arrival of a cheap, deterministic, operator-independent metering layer for verified human contribution — a certificate-transparency-style public ledger of signed human work, hash-committed, auditable, with no privileged operator able to forge or revoke entries. Whoever ships that layer captures the data-dignity economy, because they convert Lanier’s eight-year-old essay into an actual settlement system. This is precisely the gap that signed-receipt architecture targets. Universal High Income becomes mechanically possible the moment verification is cheap and the math is the trusted party — and not one day before.

**The governance hinge — and the failure mode.** The long-run outcome is not determined by capability. It is determined by who controls verification. The same proof-of-human rail that could pay a high income for verified human contribution could instead become a single chokepoint — one company, or one state, owning the authority to certify that you are human and that your work counts. China’s frontier-model ecosystem ships inside a legal regime that compels cooperation with state intelligence; that is a live reminder that a verification layer is also a surveillance layer if it is centralized. Centralized proof-of-human is not dignity. It is a permission-to-exist gate, and it is the dystopian branch of this exact technology.

The design answer is the whole point of the architecture: hash-committed constraints, a transparency log anyone can audit, no operator with the power to forge or revoke. The difference between Universal High Income and a digital ration card is not the cryptography — both use it. The difference is whether the ledger is *public and operator-independent* or *private and operator-controlled*. That single architectural choice decides whether proof-of-human pays people or imprisons them.

## 7. Conclusion

The conclusion forced by native multimodality is counterintuitive and, I think, correct. The trend that looks most like the end of human economic relevance is in fact the trend that *creates* the human provenance economy — because by collapsing the cost of synthetic output in every modality at once, it removes every hiding place except one: the verifiable fact that a real human did a specific, novel thing.

That last redoubt is not a weakness to be defended. It is the appreciating asset. Native multimodality drives the proportional term to zero and, in the same motion, drives the value of the integral term up. The humane economy on the other side does not pay people a floor for being displaced. It pays them a high income for being the one thing the accelerating machine cannot originate.

The machine changes the rate. The human still sets the setpoint. Build the ledger so the math is the trusted party, and that arrangement holds.
